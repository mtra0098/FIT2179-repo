Bushfires in Australia · 1950 to 2024

When the Continent
Burns.

Seventy years of Australian bushfire, in maps and charts.

mtra0098 2026-05-29 FIT2179 · Monash

Every summer, somewhere in Australia, the land burns. Homes turn to ash overnight, towns evacuate by the thousand, and forests carry scars that take decades to grow back. The question is no longer if the next fire season will come. It is how much worse. And the seasons that turned out worst all share one thing in common.

Bushfire is older than Australia itself. The eucalypts need it. A lot of native seeds will not open without it. People have quietly managed it on this continent for sixty thousand years.

Between 1950 and 2024 the agencies recorded enough bushfire to cover an area larger than France. The continent burnt two and a half times more land after 2000 than across the entire half-century before it. Most of the burning happened far up north and out west, in dry savanna and red spinifex where fire is just part of the year. The southeast is different. Fires there are rarer, but hotter, faster, and far more deadly, and they tend to hit where most of us live.

The charts below trace seventy restless fire seasons across maps, calendars and climate records. Geography, ignition and heat each play their part. The one piece that keeps turning up in the worst seasons, though, is rainfall: when it quietly disappears, the door swings open to the kind of summer nobody plans for.

§ 03 · Geography of fire

Where Australia burns

Western Australia alone holds a staggering 74% of all burnt area on record. Add Queensland and that share climbs to 87%. Sprawling rangeland fires in the north and west dominate the totals. The southeast burns less land but pays the heavier human cost.

§ 04 · When

Seventy fire seasons

2012 alone burnt more land than the entire 1950s, 60s and 70s combined. The post-2000 record is dominated by a small handful of brutal seasons: 2012, 2011, 2000, and 2019. Each one quietly followed a long, dry stretch.

§ 08 · Biggest fires

The biggest fires

The biggest single fire on record (Neale Junction, WA, 2018) burnt 27,696 km², larger than the entire ACT plus Greater Sydney combined. These are the ten largest individual fires in the Geoscience Australia catalogue. The catalogue treats each separate footprint as its own entry, so a season like Black Summer (which had many fronts) gets split into smaller entries instead of one giant one.

The list reads like a map of the interior. The savanna and rangelands run long, hot seasons with almost no one around to stop them, and the biggest single fires come from there. The famous southeast names (Ash Wednesday, Black Saturday) killed more people but burnt less land, so they do not show up here.

Bar length is burnt area in km². Source: Geoscience Australia HBB v2.

The thick smoke from these fires circled the entire planet. It quietly stained the glaciers of New Zealand brown by 1 January, drifted across the South Pacific to Chile and Argentina, and was detected by NASA satellites high in the stratosphere weeks later. The 33 people who died in the flames were joined by an estimated 417 more from smoke inhalation across the eastern states. By the time the rains finally came on 10 February, a charred area the size of England had burnt. Sources: Royal Commission into National Natural Disaster Arrangements (2020); Borchers Arriagada et al., MJA (2020); WWF-Australia (2020).

Black Summer did not arrive out of nowhere. The drought that fed it shows up in the climate record years earlier. The last chart in this essay pulls climate and fire onto the same page.

§ 10 · Rain and fire

When the rain fails

Rain does not write the whole story. But it writes the worst chapters.

Each dot is one year between 1990 and 2023. The horizontal axis is rainfall against the 1961 to 1990 average: dots on the right are dry years, dots on the left are wet ones. The vertical axis is the season's burnt area on a log scale.

The relationship is messier than you might expect. Plenty of wet years also burnt a lot, mostly in the savanna country up north where grass grows fast after rain and then dries out into fuel. But the dry side of the chart is where the disaster years live. 2019 was the driest year on record (190 mm below average), and it became Black Summer. 2002, the second driest year, sat above 200,000 km².

So rainfall on its own does not predict total burnt area. What it does predict is whether a season will tip from manageable into catastrophic. Hot weather, ignition sources and bad luck all play their parts. Rain is the variable that decides whether those parts add up to a typical season or a once-in-a-generation one.

When the rain does not come, the worst-case scenarios get unlocked. The fire seasons we used to plan around are not the fire seasons we are getting.

Looking ahead to 2026. The Bureau of Meteorology's latest outlook suggests a drier-than-average winter and spring across southeastern Australia, with rainfall sitting in the lowest 30% of historical records for parts of NSW and Victoria. CSIRO's State of the Climate report projects fire weather days to keep climbing across the south through the late 2020s.

If the pattern in the chart above holds, those rainfall figures should worry us. The conditions that fed Black Summer are not exceptional anymore; they are the new shape of the summer. The next catastrophic year is not a matter of if. It is a matter of which.
