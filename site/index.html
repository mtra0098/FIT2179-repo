Seventy years of Australian bushfire, in maps and charts.

mtra0098 2026-05-29 8 min read FIT2179 · Monash

Every summer the country burns somewhere. This is what the public data shows about where, when, and why.

Bushfire is older than Australia itself. The eucalypts need it. A lot of native seeds will not open without it. People have been managing it on this continent for sixty thousand years.

Between 1950 and 2024 the agencies recorded enough bushfire to cover an area bigger than France. Most of it was up north and out west, in the savannas of the Top End and the spinifex of the Pilbara, where fire is just part of the year. The southeast is different. Fires there are rarer, but they are hotter and faster, and they tend to hit where most of us live.

The charts below come from three places: the Bureau of Meteorology, Geoscience Australia, and Digital Earth Australia. Scroll through and have a look. The thing to keep in mind is whether the fire season we used to plan around is still the fire season we are getting.

Where heat meets fire

How to read this map

This map shows two things at once. The colour says how often a state burns (up the legend) and how much hotter its summers have been getting (across the legend). The darker the state, the worse on both counts.

WA, the NT and Queensland share the same dark signature. That is the savanna fire belt: hot, and burning often.

The southeast (Victoria, southern NSW, the ACT) sits in the middle of the legend. Fires there are less common, but the summers are hot and most of the population lives there.

§ 08 · Biggest fires

The biggest fires

This is the ten largest single fires in the Geoscience Australia record. The catalogue treats each separate fire footprint as its own entry, so a season like Black Summer (which had many fronts) gets split into a lot of smaller entries instead of one giant one.

The list reads like a map of the interior. The savanna and the rangelands run long, hot seasons with few people around, and the biggest single fires come from there. The famous southeast names (Ash Wednesday, Black Saturday) killed more people but burnt less land, so they do not show up here.

Bar length is burnt area in km². Source: Geoscience Australia HBB v2.

§ 09 · Black summer

Black Summer, day by day

Number of new fires each day across Australia, 1 July 2019 to 31 March 2020. Every cell is one day. Columns are weeks. Rows are days of the week.

By 4 January the season had already burnt over 5.5 million hectares in NSW alone. The dark band you can see through late December and January isn't one fire. It's lots of separate fronts going at once, from the Snowy Mountains up to the Mid North Coast, on days over 40°C with mountain wind pouring down the slopes.

§ 10 · Rain and fire

When the rain fails

The worst fire years sit on the dry side of the chart.

Each dot is one year. Going left to right is rainfall compared to the 1961 to 1990 average: dots on the right are dry years, dots on the left are wet ones. Up the y-axis is the year's burnt area on a log scale, so each gridline is roughly a doubling.

Follow the line as a path. It wanders through the wet 1990s and the drying 2000s, then in 2019 it jumps almost straight up. The driest year on BOM's record gave us the biggest fire year on Geoscience Australia's record.

The pattern is simple: when the rain doesn't come through winter and spring, the next summer pays for it.

Methodology
  • Annual totals pulled straight from GA HBB v2, grouped by ignition year. Overlapping polygons are not dissolved, so cumulative areas are slightly inflated.
  • Numbers before 1990 mix older state agency archives with later satellite data, so the early record is rougher.
  • Maps use an Albers equal-area projection (lat₀ = -18°, lat₁ = -36°) so state areas can be compared fairly.
